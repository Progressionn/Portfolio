Reflective Essay on My Minor Experience

As an aspiring English language teacher in international schools, this Minor has equipped me with foundational pedagogical tools while revealing areas for growth. My long-term plan is to work as an English teacher in international schools while also developing as an educational content creator. This dual path appeals to me because it combines classroom interaction with creative freedom, allowing me to impact learners both directly and through digital platforms basing on experiential learning, which aligns with modern communicative approaches.

Educational Practices

During the Minor, I learned several effective educational scenarios that will help me achieve my goals. Three key practices I have adopted are:

  1. Task-Based Learning from Didactics, where I designed a giving directions role-play observing immediate student engagement;
  2. Scaffolding techniques from Educational Psychology, helping me break down complex grammar into digestible steps during peer teaching;
  3. Peer Feedback Systems from Classroom Assessment where I studied about "delayed feedback" and "correction on the spot" techniques.

Professional Strengths

My strengths include:

  1. Cultural sensitivity from volunteering with multilingual learners, allowing me to spot linguistic interference patterns;
  2. Adaptive design of the content with the help of which I can modify the materials for different proficiency levels;
  3. Technology integration, which supports vocabulary and grammar reviews with notably higher retention rates compared to traditional methods.

Analysis

1. The main audience

  1. People who care about their health: sauerkraut is a fermented product that contains probiotics and is often associated with digestive benefits. Thus, people who prefer a healthy lifestyle and are interested in gut health can become a key target.
  2. Gourmets and culinary enthusiasts: those who like to experiment with different flavors and cuisines may be interested in sauerkraut as a versatile and unique ingredient in various dishes.
  3. Those who are interested in traditional or international cuisine: sauerkraut is a traditional dish of many European cuisines, especially German and Eastern European. People interested in authentic or international dishes may like sauerkraut.
  4. Vegans and vegetarians: sauerkraut is often prepared on a plant-based basis and it can be a suitable addition to a vegetarian diet.
  5. Consumers who need natural and fermented foods: due to the growing popularity of fermented foods due to their potential health benefits, advertisements for sauerkraut may be targeted at consumers who are looking for natural, minimally processed and fermented foods.
  6. People who are looking for low-calorie, low-fat meals: sauerkraut tends to be low in calories and fat, which makes it attractive to people who monitor their calorie and fat intake.

2. The register

Informal.

  • Field: in our sauerkraut advertisement, the field is "food and culinary products", with an emphasis on promoting sauerkraut.
  • Tenor includes the relationship between the advertiser (the one who promotes sauerkraut) and the target audience. It influences the language and tone used in advertising, aimed at persuading and engaging the potential consumers.
  • Mode: spoken, remote (a pre-recorded video) and synchronous.

The language used in the advertisement reflects the characteristics of the food industry with an emphasis on persuading and convincing the audience to consider sauerkraut as a desirable and healthy product. The tone emphasizes the health benefits, culinary versatility and cultural authenticity.

3. Genre features

The genre of the text is a persuasive advertisement.

Layout: the text is structured as a conversation between an interviewer and celebrities, followed by a narrator providing information about the product.

Content:

  • Potential problems which may be familiar to the audience are brought up (having to cook for guests, lacking energy for everyday activities, etc.) and a solution is provided ("You won't ever experience that again as long as sauerkraut is included in your diet")
  • Celebrity endorsements: the use of quotes from celebrities adds credibility and persuasiveness to the claims about sauerkraut.
  • Scientific evidence: the text mentions scientific research to support the claims about the nutritional value of sauerkraut.

Linguistic features:

  • Direct speech is used in the beginning of the ad.
  • Rhetorical questions: the narrator asks, "Who said it is one apple a day that keeps the doctor away?" to challenge the traditional belief and introduce sauerkraut as a superior alternative.
  • Descriptive emotional language: the text uses words like "rich," "essential," "improves," "enhances," and "delights" to emphasize the positive qualities and benefits of sauerkraut.
  • Imperatives: the text uses imperative verbs like "Discover," "Try," and "Enjoy" to encourage the reader to take action and try sauerkraut.

4. Cohesive devices

  1. Referencing enhances the text's cohesion by connecting ideas and providing continuity.
    Anaphoric reference
    - "It (sauerkraut) is full of vitamins A, B, C and necessary microelements."
    - "You won't ever experience that (feeling worn out and stressed) again as long as sauerkraut is included in your diet."
    Cataphoric reference
    - "Who said it (one apple a day) is one apple a day that keeps the doctor away?."
    - "It was proven by scientific research that eating only 100 grams of sauerkraut a day can provide you with a daily supply of vitamins!"
  2. Linking phrases create a coherent link between two sentences.
    "On top of that, one serving contains 3 grams of fiber that enhances weight loss and digestion."
  3. Conjunction relates sentences and clauses to each other.
    - Subordinating conjunctions
    "Enjoy it in summer and winter, whether you're sunbathing on the beach or skiing down a mountain slope."
    - Coordinating conjunctions
    "And just listen to the crunch!"
    "Enjoy it in summer and winter, whether you're sunbathing on the beach or skiing down a mountain slope."
  4. Ellipsis involves the omission of words or phrases that can be inferred from the context. It helps maintain a concise and fluent writing style.
    "And just listen to the crunch! (It's) Like music to your ears."
  5. Comparative cohesion: the comparison between sauerkraut and an apple emphasizes the superior health benefits of sauerkraut.
    "Who said it is one apple a day that keeps the doctor away? Rich both in taste and vitamins, sauerkraut will become an essential part of your diet."
  6. Synonymy is used in the text to explain the meaning of the word "sauerkraut".
    "Fermented Cabbage aka *SAUERKRAUT*"
  7. Collocations are words that tend to go together:
    - "Hollywood smile" - this collocation is used to describe a perfect and attractive smile, often associated with celebrities.
    - "Health and well-being" - this is a common collocation that refers to a person's overall physical and mental health.
    - "Secret ingredient" - this collocation is used to describe something that is believed to be the key or special element in achieving a certain result.

5. Syntactic features

  • Future Simple Tense (Rich both in taste and vitamins, sauerkraut will become an essential part of your diet).
  • Present Progressive Tense (No need to worry about what to serve when you're having guests around).
  • A simple sentence (You can never go wrong with sauerkraut.)
  • A complex sentence (You won't ever experience that again as long as sauerkraut is included in your diet.)
  • Subject - NP one serving (On top of that, one serving contains 3 grams of fiber that enhances weight loss and digestion.)
  • A transitive regular verb - enhance (enhances weight loss and digestion.)
  • A phrasal verb - keep away (Who said it's one apple a day that keeps the doctor away)
  • An ellipsis ([There is] No need to worry about what to serve when you're having guests around.)
  • A coordinate clause (Sauerkraut fills you with energy and improves your mood so there is no chance you miss out on work or doing things you enjoy.)
  • A subordinate clause (Think of all the times you couldn't excel at work or relish your favorite activities because you felt worn out or stressed).

6. Potential problems with syntactic features

Tense: Present Continuous ('...when you're having guests around')

  • Meaning: a pre-intermediate student might not feel the context of an ongoing/background action and does not understand the reasons for using Present Continuous;
  • Form: a student of a pre-intermediate level or lower might use Present Simple tense (*when you have guests around), basing their choice on the knowledge that stative words are usually not used in progressive aspect;
  • Meaning: considering the point mentioned above, students should be informed that some verbs can occur in the progressive aspect with a difference in meaning. For instance, 'have' can be used in Present Continuous not with a meaning of 'possession' but referring to some action or experience;
  • Pronunciation: pre-intermediate students might pronounce a full form of pronoun and auxiliary verb instead of a contraction (/juː ɑːr/ vs. /jər/).

Clauses: a relative dependent clause ('Who said it's one apple a day that keeps the doctor away?')

  • Punctuation: intermediate students might not distinguish a defining relative clause (information cannot be omitted, no commas needed) from a non-defining one (information is not necessary, commas needed). That is why, here they can put a comma or make mistakes in other cases where it is difficult to determine the type of clause.
  • Punctuation: relative pronoun that does not require the usage of commas. Probably, some students of lower levels can make mistakes not realizing that;
  • Form of a verb: a relative pronoun that is not explicitly marked in terms of a number and pre-intermediate students can omit -s affix in verb since they do not match the pronoun with the word in singular number (*that keep the doctor away);
  • Form: intermediate level students can put this relative pronoun in the sentences all the time since they might lack the knowledge of the fact that relative pronoun that can be omitted without any change to the meaning.

Grammar Language Analysis

TARGET LANGUAGE

What is the language point?

The First Conditional

Example(s):

1. If you eat too much cake, you will feel sick.

2. If they invite us to the party, we will definitely go.

3. If I see her at the store, I will tell her the news.

4. If the weather is nice, we will have a picnic in the park.
Meaning analysis

It's essential to analyze the meaning behind the structure to help students understand when and how to use it. Here's a possible way to conduct a meaning analysis for the first Conditional:

1. Focusing on what does the first Conditional express:

  • Explain that the first Conditional is used to talk about real or possible future situations that depend on a certain condition.

2. Sentence Structure:

  • Discuss the structure: If + present simple, will + base verb.
  • Explain that the if-clause presents a condition, and the main clause expresses the result of this condition.
  • Example: "If I study (condition), I will pass the exam (result)."

3. Indicating real condition:

  • Emphasize that the condition must be realistic. For example, "If I win the lottery" suggests an uncommon but possible situation, while "If I fly to the moon" does not fit as a real possibility.

Remark: on the initial steps it is worth focusing on the general meaning of the first Conditional while not considering all possible meanings at once (e.g. suggestions, warnings) in order not to overload students with too much information.
Elicitation: meaning

Context:

If it's sunny this weekend, I will go to the beach with my friends. We will swim and play volleyball all afternoon. If we get tired, we will have a picnic on the sand and enjoy some snacks. If the weather is nice, I am sure we will have a great time. However, if it rains, we will stay indoors and watch movies instead.

Elicitation question(s):

1. If it's sunny, what will I do?
- (Expected answer: I will go to the beach.)

2. What will we do if we get tired at the beach?
- (Expected answer: We will have a picnic on the sand.)

3. What happens if it rains?
- (Expected answer: We will stay indoors and watch movies.)
Concept checking

Concept Checking Questions:

1. Is the speaker talking about a past or future event?
- (Expected answer: Future).

2. If I say "If it's sunny this weekend, I will go to the beach with my friends," does this mean that I will definitely go to the beach with my friends?"
- (Expected answer: No, it means it's a possibility.)

3. Is going to the beach certain?
- (Expected answer: No, it depends on whether it is sunny.)

4. If it rains, will we stay indoors and watch movies?
- (Expected answer: Yes, but only if it rains — we are unsure about it now.)

5. Does the first part of the sentence (if it rains) express a real possibility?
- (Expected answer: Yes, it refers to a real possible situation.)
Form

Affirmative form:

If we have time, we will (we'll) see our friends.
If + subject + V1, subject + will + infinitive

or

We will (we'll) see our friends, if we have time.
Subject + will + infinitive, if + subject + V1.

Negative form:

If we don't have time, we won't see our friends.
If + subject + don't (doesn't) + V1, subject + won't + infinitive

Question:

Will we see our friends if we have time?
Will + subject + infinitive, if + subject + V1?

Negative question:

Won't we see our friends if we don't have time?
Won't + subject + infinitive, if + subject + don't (doesn't) V1?

Remark: there are obviously more ways to form the first Conditional (e.g. If you don't leave now, you will miss the train), but due to the fact that I teach pre-intermediate students it is worth focusing on these basic patterns of forming the first Conditional on the initial steps.
Pronunciation

When forming both affirmative and negative statements it is more natural to use a contraction (e.g. affirmative form: we'll = we will, he'll = he will; negative form: won't).

In natural speech words connect smoothly:

If_we have time, we'll see our friends. ([ɪf wɪ hæv] -- sounds linked).

Weak pronunciation of have/has in if-clause: if we have time → [ɪf wɪ həv taɪm] (the h in "have/has" may disappear).

If often links smoothly with the subject that follows it:

(e.g. If we → [ɪfwi];
If you → [ɪfju];
If I → [ɪfaɪ];
If he → [ɪfhi];
If it → [ɪfɪt]).

In the affirmative statements intonation usually falls in the if-clause and rises in the main clause (If we have time ↘, we'll see our friends ↗).

When a question starts with "will", intonation rises first and then falls:

Will we see our friends if we have time? (↗Will we see our friends if we have time↘?).

ANTICIPATED PROBLEMS & SOLUTIONS: MEANING

Problem 1: some students may believe that the first Conditional expresses a 100% guaranteed future event rather than a probability.

Solution 1: clarify that the first Conditional expresses probability, rather than certainty by providing additional examples where the first Conditional is used.

Problem 2: students may assume that "if" always indicates a first Conditional sentence, even though "if" can also be used outside of the first Conditional (e.g. If you want, you can join us).

Solution 2: writing on the board two sentences that illustrate differences in the use of "if" in the first Conditional and in other contexts and explain differences in meaning.

e.g. If you study hard, you will pass the exam.
If you want, you can take a break.

Problem 3: learners may not understand that the result in the main clause depends on the condition in the if-clause (they might think that both clauses describe independent future actions).

Solution 3: write examples with the first Conditional on the board and mark relationships between the clauses (e.g. drawing arrows from if-clause to the main clause).
ANTICIPATED PROBLEMS & SOLUTIONS: FORM

Problem 1: students might forget to change the form of the verb in the if-clause supposing that they need an infinitive there (e.g. if he have time, he'll see his friends instead of if he has time, he'll see his friends).

Solution 1: practice the same sentence with different subjects in order to prevent mistakes such as forgetting to change the form of the verb in if-clause (e.g. pay attention to sentences with he/she subjects where ending -s is necessary).

Problem 2: learners may use the verb will in the if-clause instead of the main clause.

Solution 2: make a board record and highlight if and will within if-clause and pay attention to the present form of the verb within the main clause (e.g. square brackets can be used to show the boundary between the if-clause and the main clause).

Problem 3: students often forget to use the correct word order in the question form.

Solution 3: provide students with additional sets of exercises to practice the correct word order in the question forms (ensure that "will" comes before the subject in questions).
ANTICIPATED PROBLEMS & SOLUTIONS: PHONOLOGY

Problem 1: for some students it may be difficult to correctly pronounce the verb "will" in connected speech since in natural speech subject + will should be pronounced as a contraction.

Solution 1: highlight on the board one more time the difference between the pronunciation of we will and we'll and give students more exercises to reinforce their memory. [wi wɪl] [wiːl]

Problem 2: the word "if" might be pronounced unclearly or not linked properly with the next word.

Solution 2: practice the reduced form of "if" (/ɪf/), especially when it's linked with the following word (e.g. "if we" → [ɪfwi]); focus on smooth and connected pronunciation to sound more natural.

Problem 3: students may forget to change intonation in the question forms and make it the same as in affirmatives.

Solution 3: make an emphasis on the differences in intonation between affirmative and question forms by writing both an affirmative and a question form on the board and marking the rising and the falling intonation; after that complete a series of exercises trying to pronounce the sentences with appropriate intonation.

Self-designed Learning Project

"Giving Directions While Traveling"

Step 1: Identify Your Learner Group

Age: 18-25 years old (young adults)

Level: Pre-intermediate-intermediate (A2-B1)

Number of students: 14 (working in pairs)

Interests: Travel, city exploration, navigation, and interactive communication.

This age group is chosen for this project because young adults in this range often travel independently or plan trips abroad, making giving and understanding directions a highly practical skill. At the A2-B1 level, students can form basic sentences but usually struggle with fluency, logical sequencing, and natural linking phrases — key elements this project targets.

The task is designed for pair work (7 pairs total) to maximize speaking time and peer interaction. Since each dialogue will last approximately 1-2 minutes, even with 7 pairs, there will be enough time for feedback and discussion.

Step 2: Project Theme, Task & Final Product

The project's theme is going to be "Giving Directions While Traveling". The project focuses on giving and understanding directions in English. This topic was chosen because it aligns with the real-life needs of students, who, as young adults, may travel, study abroad, or interact with tourists in their own cities. After learning key vocabulary items (e.g., turn left, go straight, it's next to) and practicing simple directions in class, students will apply their skills in a pair role-play activity.

Each pair will receive different scenario cards: one student plays a lost tourist, and the other acts as a helpful local. They must create a 1-2 minute dialogue where the tourist asks for directions to a landmark (e.g., a train station, museum, or café), and the local responds using clear, step-by-step instructions with linking words (e.g., First, Then, Finally). After all pairs perform, the class will vote on the clearest and most natural-sounding directions, reinforcing practical communication skills.

The final product (a spoken dialogue) mirrors real-world interactions, building confidence for travel. This task encourages active listening, as students must react to their partner's directions (e.g., "Sorry, could you repeat that?"). The competitive element (voting) keeps the activity engaging and interactive.

Step 3: Learning Outcomes

By the end of the project, students will be able to:

  • Give clear directions using linking phrases (First, Next, Then, Finally).
  • Ask for directions politely ("Excuse me, how do I get to...?").
  • Understand and follow spoken directions.
  • Role-play a travel scenario naturally.

By practicing these skills, students will develop real-world communicative competence for travel situations while improving their fluency. The role-play format encourages quick thinking and adaptation, mirroring authentic interactions where directions might need to be simplified or repeated. Additionally, this activity will help students build cultural awareness — students learn that giving directions varies across countries (e.g., landmarks vs. street names in some cultures). The collaborative nature of pair work also fosters patience and teamwork, as students must listen carefully and support each other's learning.

Step 4: Assessment Criteria

  • Clarity of directions (logical sequence).
  • Use of linking phrases (e.g., First, Then, Finally).
  • Pronunciation & fluency (no long pauses).
  • The correct use of prepositions ("Turn left at the..." vs. "Turn left on the...").
  • Engagement (natural tone, eye contact).
  • Following the time limit (no more than 2 minutes).

Step 5: Equipment Needed

  • Printed location cards (Tourist & Local versions).
  • City map visuals (e.g., Tokyo, Rome, etc.).
  • Whiteboard and markers (for modeling directions).
  • Timer (for dialogue practice).

Step 6: Support Needed

  • Vocabulary on the topic of travelling which will be used in the dialogue.
  • Pre-teach direction phrases ("Go straight," "Turn left/right," "It's next to...").
  • Review linking words (First, Next, After that, Finally).
  • Model a sample dialogue.
  • Allow rehearsal time before performing.

Step 7: Model Dialogue (Procedure)

I will begin by demonstrating a sample dialogue between a tourist and a local, showing the format students should follow.

After that I will highlight the features to which students should pay attention while completing the task:

  • Structure: Clear sequencing with linking words (First, Then, After that, Finally).
  • Landmarks: Using visible reference points (fountain, supermarket, park).
  • Polite language: "Excuse me," "Of course," "You can't miss it".
  • Prepositions: "along," "onto," "on your left/right".
  • Natural flow: No long pauses, conversational tone.

After modeling, I will ask students to identify these features in the sample dialogue and explain why each is important for clear directions. We will then practice chorally repeating key phrases before students create their own dialogues. This will help ensure everyone understands the expectations before beginning pair work.

“Global Fusion Festival: a multicultural language celebration”

Description of the educational context

The Global Fusion Festival is designed to create an immersive language-learning experience that bridges classroom instruction with real-world cultural exploration. Set in our language school's vibrant international environment, this event provides students with a unique opportunity to practice their target language (English, French, or Spanish) in authentic, interactive scenarios. By engaging with diverse cultural traditions, music, and customs, participants will naturally develop their listening and speaking skills while expanding their vocabulary related to travel, cuisine, and social interactions. The activities are carefully structured to align with CEFR levels A2-B2, ensuring accessibility for intermediate learners while offering enough challenge to keep advanced students engaged. This festival represents our school’s commitment to fostering not just linguistic proficiency but also genuine cultural appreciation and global citizenship.

Description of the event

The Global Fusion Festival transforms our school into a bustling multicultural hub through four interactive activity stations. At the Language Café, students will practice ordering food in their target language from country-themed booths representing France, Mexico, Great Britain and others. The Music & Dance Challenge station invites participants to learn short song lyrics or dance moves while discovering cultural context. For creative writing practice, the Postcard Exchange allows students to compose messages to fictional characters using newly-learned cultural facts. Perhaps most exciting is the Debate Zone, where learners discuss lighthearted topics like "Is pizza a sandwich?" entirely in their target language. Throughout the event, students collect passport stamps at each station, working toward prizes while naturally reinforcing their language skills. Optional cultural dress-up (berets, sombreros, etc.) and a special guest speaker session with a local expat sharing travel stories add to the immersive experience.

Target Student Group

This festival is ideally suited for:

  • Language learners at CEFR levels A2 (Pre-Intermediate) to B2 (Upper-Intermediate);
  • Students aged 12-18 (though easily adaptable for adult learners);
  • Classes focusing on general language acquisition or specific exam preparation (IELTS, DELF, etc.);
  • Students who benefit from kinesthetic and social learning styles;

The activities are designed to be inclusive yet challenging enough to push students slightly beyond their comfort zones in a supportive environment.

Purpose of the event

The Global Fusion Festival serves three primary objectives:

Linguistic Development: By removing classroom walls, students practice spontaneous communication through unscripted interactions like food ordering and informal debates, significantly improving fluency and listening comprehension.

Cultural Enrichment: Exposure to diverse traditions through music, cuisine and customs fosters genuine intercultural understanding that textbooks cannot provide.

Social-Emotional Growth: The low-pressure, game-like atmosphere helps reduce language anxiety while collaborative tasks build teamwork and public speaking confidence.

These goals align perfectly with modern language pedagogy emphasizing communicative competence and intercultural awareness.

  • Pre-event vocabulary lists (e.g., polite ordering phrases);
